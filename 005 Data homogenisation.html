Defining homogenisation

The Oxford English Dictionary offers two definitions of homogenise:

  1. Subject (milk) to a process in which the fat droplets are emulsified and the cream does not separate:
  2. Make uniform or similar.

With the term data homogenisation we are using the second definition. Digital technologies make data "uniform or similar".

Data homogenisation explained

Whether a digital technology is allowing you to talk to friends via Skype (or smartphone or...); capture images of snow monkeys; listen to Charlie Parker; measure the temperature; analyse the social interactions in a discussion forum; or, put your students to sleep as you read from your powerpoint slides (which they're viewing via some lecture capture system) all of the data is represented as a combination of zeros and ones. All the data is digital. All the data has been made "uniform or similar". It has been homogenised.

The result of this is that all digital technologies are designed to manipulate 0s and 1s (digital data). This means that, in theory at least, all digital technologies can handle all data. The content has been separated from the medium (Yoo, Henfridsson & Lyytinen, 2010).

When data is not homogenised

Analog technologies (technologies that are not digital), on the other hand, have a tight coupling between content and medium. Way back in the 1980s I bought "Born in the USA" on a vinyl record. When I eventually purchased a https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walkman Walkman I could use the vinyl record. I had to transfer the content from the vinyl record onto a cassette tape that I could play on my Walkman. I had to transfer the content into a different format. A different medium, required a different format for the content.

Another example of data homogenisation

Everyone knows and has probably used Wikipedia. Did you know that Wikipedia maintains a list of all recent changes made to Wikipedia? It lists which page was changed, what was changed, the type of change, when it happened, and who made the change. Given the huge number of people editing Wikipedia at any one time there are always new recent changes.

Required resources

Visit the Wikipedia recent changes page and look at the most recent changes. Now refresh or reload the page. Did you see a change? Refresh it again.

This page shows a different representation of the recent changes that is updated live.

Link to data homogenisation

The data being displayed is stored somewhere as binary numbers (i.e. collections of zeros and ones). Remember, all data with digital technologies is reduced to binary numbers. That's data homogenisation.

The recent changes page is showing that data as a list of text. The digital technology has looked at the binary numbers and translated it into text that we can read.

However, while we can read it, it's not the best way the data could be represented. Especially, if our aim is to get a feel for how often Wikipedia is changing and how.

Looking for a better representation

Do you think it would be easier to follow if this information was represented using images and sound? What if the images we quite relaxing to watch, especially if it was combined with ambient music?

Given the combination of data homogenisation and reprogramability (looking at this next) properties of digital technology, it is possible to manipulate data and represent it in different forms.

Data homogenisation and the Digital technologies subject

The combination of data homogenisation and reprogrammability (more on this next) - the fundamental properties of digital technologies - provide digital technologies with its true power to collect, manage, manipulate and analyse data of just about anything. Not surprisingly these form a key part of the digital technologies subject.
