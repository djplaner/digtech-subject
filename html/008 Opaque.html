Opaque

Explains the opaque nature of digital technologies and establishes some links with the Digital technologies subject.

Introduction

Koehler and Mishra (2009) draw on Turkle (1995) to define opaque as "the inner workings are hidden from users". Turkle (1995) talks about people having "become accustomed to opaque technology". Meaning that as the power of digital technologies have increased we no longer see the inner workings of the technology as they've become hidden behind graphical user interfaces.

This is important because of reprogrammability.

Every digital technology is programed. It has been designed to work a certain way. If you understand how the digital technology is programmed, you can predict how it will work, you can problem solve with it. Ben-Ari and Yeshno (2006) found that people with appropriate conceptual models of digital technologies were better able to analyse and solve problems. While learners without appropriate conceptual models were limited to aimless trial and error; and, had higher levels of anxiety.

It causes anxiety

It appears that much of the anxiety people feel about digital technologies is due to the opaque nature of digital technology. The fact that what you see of the digital technology does not help you understand how it is programmed and how it should work.

A lot of the tension people feel around digital technologies are not due to their lack of knowledge, it's due to poorly designed interfaces and software.

Importance to the Digital technologies subject

Understanding that digital technologies tend toward the opaque and that this impacts how and what people can do with digital technologies links to a number of key aims for the Digital technologies subject. But perhaps the most obvious is this aim

use computational thinking and the key concepts of abstraction; data collection, representation and interpretation; specification, algorithms and implementation to create digital solutions

Opaque digital technologies are examples of poor abstraction and representation, often due to poorly designed specifications and algorithms and will eventually lead to digital solutions that are poorly implemented.

The value of transparency (not opaque)

The problem with opaque digital technologies is that it's not clear how they work. This might be due to a bad abstraction (how the algorithms underlying the program are designed) or a bad representation (how the interface of the program is designed).

The following is intended to show you the difference having the right abstraction (algorithm) and representation can make. It aims to show how much easier and more productive if the model underpinning a task is transparent, especially if it allows the person to draw upon existing knowledge.

Good digital technologies are not opaque

The point is that it is only badly designed digital technologies that are opaque. A well-designed digital technology will be transparent to use and actually magnify the person's capability.

In the Digital technologies subject it is important that students learn this point and work toward an understanding of what helps make a digital technology transparent.
