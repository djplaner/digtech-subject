Unstable

A look at what the unstable nature of digital technology means and any connection to the Digital technologies subject.

Introduction

The nature of digital technologies

We now move from the fundamental properties of digital technologies (data homogenisation and reprogrammability) onto the nature of digital technologies as described by Koehler and Mishra (2009) who identified digital technologies as: unstable, opaque, and protean. We'll start with unstable in this book. The next two books will focus on opaque and protean.

Unstable

Koehler and Mishra (2009) define unstable as "rapidly changing" (p. 61). Unstable is perhaps the feature of digital technology that is most familiar to you (it's also the one least discussed within the Digital technologies subject). An example of the rapidly changing nature of digital technology is the observation from earlier in this book that most contemporary cars have over 50 microcontrollers, each of which is more powerful than the single guidance computer on the Apollo moon landings. One example of unstable digital technology

Then

The following image is of a IBM Model 350 Disk File from 1956. The equivalent of a hard-drive. The device included 50 24-inch discs, weighed 1 ton, and could hold a "massive" 5Mb (you'll see just how small that is soon).

You didn't buy the computer it was attached to, you had to lease the computer for $USD3,200 a month. To give you some idea of how expensive that is, the average price for a new house in the United States in 1956 was $USD11,700.

Now

60 years later, the following image is the best-selling USB stick on the USA-based Amazon site. For $USD7.99 you can purchase a "drive" that will hold 32Gb of information and weighs around 20 grams. Just how much of a change that is, is demonstrated on the following page.

Connecting unstable to the Digital technologies subject

The following content description from the Digital technologies subject

Acquire, store and validate different types of data, and use a range of software to interpret and visualise data to create information (ACTDIP016)

This content description expects students to be able to perform do a range of tasks with data, including being able to visualise data to create information. This page includes details and resources for a research project that is "designed for year 6 students to investigate and present information about an emerging digital technology".

Note: it makes of the "Big6" 6-stage model designed to help "anyone solve problems or make decisions by using information". A potential pedagogical approach that might be useful.

Let's use this as an exercise to illustrate the unstable nature of digital technology.

Chances are you can't even see the bar for the 1956 storage device. You might be able to see if you download and open the Excel spreadsheet I used.

The unstable nature of digital technology has created a change so vast that it is difficult to visualise. The 2016 simple USB stick can hold 6400 times more data than then 1 ton 1956 device.

As a comparison, Saturn has a diameter only 9.5 times bigger than Earth. You could fit 764 planets like Earth into Saturn.

But wait there is more...

You can currently purchase high capacity USB drives that will hold 200,000 times the data as the 1956 device.

Hints:

  • For Questions 1 and 2 you might like to treat the Storage example in the spreadsheet as an algorithm that has the following steps
    1. Present the complete data
    2. Convert the numbers into the same unit (e.g. change storage capacity figures so they are both in Mb; change the weights so they are both in grams)
    3. Create a table with just the year and the data you're interested in.
    4. Create a chart with Excel of that data

    A simple way to do this would be to create a new sheet and copy and paste the content from the storage sheet.
